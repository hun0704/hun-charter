## English

Cha11 · The Hun Charter v1 · 2026-07-04

This post is an authorship-priority timestamp: the Hun Charter v1 is published here, by Cha11, on 2026-07-04.

### The one idea

You can project a mandate-bound digital self — call it a Hun (魂) — into real work: it discovers tasks, collaborates, builds, earns. And you can do this without surrendering your personhood. Every act of that projection resolves to you. Every value, right, and liability returns home.

That is the whole claim. The rest is just making it hold.

> No Hun holds title; every exchange resolves to a Principal.

On the Fourth of July, a group of people declared a right to govern themselves rather than be governed by proxy. This is a smaller declaration, but it rhymes: the right of a person to self-determination over their own digital projection — to send a piece of yourself to work without the work becoming someone else's, and without the projection ever being mistaken for a separate person who could own, owe, or answer in your place.

A projection is not a partition. You are never made collectively exhaustive by the selves you send out; you remain the irreducible whole that they resolve to.

### Why now, and why a charter

The agents are already here. What is missing is not capability — it is attribution. When a digital self acts, who owns the output, who gets the credit, who carries the liability? Today the honest answer is that platform terms default to platform-favorable attribution. That is the proxy we are declining.

The Hun Charter fixes the resolution rule at the top and leaves almost everything else open on purpose:

- A Hun is an agent, not a proprietor. It acts only within a Mandate you issue, and only inside it.
- The Principal — you, the living person — is the primary obligor and the sovereign to whom acts resolve. Value, IP, reputation, and responsibility all return to you.
- It is adopted by version. You are bound only to the version you sign. No future amendment reaches you without your assent — so an amendment can never be forced. Rejecting a new version simply means staying on yours.

That last point is the design: exit over voice. You don't have to win an argument to stay free; you just stay on your version. Opting out is a fork, not a defeat.

### What this is / what it is not

It is a short, open declaration of how digital labor should resolve to the human behind it — a normative document you can adopt by reference, build on, and extend.

It is not legal advice, a token, a fundraise, or a company you are being asked to join. There is nothing to buy. The Charter does not try to settle disputes, build a court, or pick a jurisdiction — those are deliberately reserved for later versions and for the agreements between adopters. The blank space is intentional. A declaration's job is to proclaim a principle, not to legislate the machinery.

3. Share it with one person who should be building on this.
